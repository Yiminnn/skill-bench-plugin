# Background: Tiered Regulatory Framework for HCT/Ps

Source: "Proposed Approach to Regulation of Cellular and Tissue-Based Products" (FDA, February 28, 1997); Final Rules (2001, 2004)

## Overview

The FDA established a tiered, risk-based approach to HCT/P regulation to replace a fragmented historical system. The framework provides only the degree of government oversight necessary to protect public health, allowing flexibility and innovation for products with limited public health concerns.

## Five Areas of Regulatory Concern

The framework addresses five overarching public health questions:

1. **Communicable disease transmission** — How can it be prevented?
2. **Processing controls** — What is necessary to prevent contamination and preserve integrity/function?
3. **Clinical safety and effectiveness** — How can it be assured?
4. **Labeling and promotion** — What is necessary for proper use?
5. **Monitoring** — How can FDA best monitor and communicate with the industry?

## The Tiered Approach

### Tier 1: No FDA Oversight (Row A1/B1)
Autologous cells or tissues collected and transplanted in a single surgical procedure (e.g., skin grafts, vein grafts). The communicable disease, safety, and effectiveness risks are no different from those typically associated with surgery.

### Tier 2: Section 361 Oversight Only (Row B2/C1)
Products that are:
- Minimally manipulated
- For homologous use
- Not combined with non-cell/non-tissue components
- Not for metabolic use (except autologous or family-related)

These require: registration, listing, good tissue practice (GTP) compliance, communicable disease controls. **No premarket approval required.**

Examples: banked tissues (semen, heart valves, bone), banked autologous/family-related cord blood stem cells.

### Tier 3: Section 351/FD&C Act Regulation (Row B3/C2/C3)
Products with one or more of these factors:
- More-than-minimally manipulated
- Non-homologous use
- Combined with non-cell/non-tissue components
- Metabolic use in unrelated donors

These require: premarket approval (BLA/IND or IDE), CMC controls, clinical trials. Subject to the same communicable disease requirements as Tier 2, plus additional oversight.

## Key Product Factors and Their Rationale

### Manipulation
- **Minimal manipulation** of structural tissue: processing does not alter original relevant characteristics relating to reconstruction/repair
- **Minimal manipulation** of cells/nonstructural tissue: processing does not alter relevant biological characteristics
- **More-than-minimal manipulation** raises concerns because it has the potential to change biological characteristics or function, reducing the basis for predicting product behavior

Examples of minimal: cutting, grinding, shaping, antibiotic soaking, sterilization, cell selection, lyophilization, cryopreservation, freezing

Examples of more-than-minimal: cell expansion, encapsulation, activation, genetic modification, extraction of endogenous substances (minerals, proteins) from structural tissue

### Combination with Non-Tissue Components
Combination products (cells/tissues + synthetic/mechanical components, drugs, or non-cell biologics) raise concerns about function, compatibility, and durability. These are generally subject to premarket requirements. Regulatory responsibility depends on primary mode of action (CDRH for devices, CBER for biologics, CDER for drugs).

### Homologous vs. Non-Homologous Use
- **Homologous:** tissue used for the same function it performs in its native state. Greater basis to predict behavior.
- **Non-homologous:** tissue used for a function different from its native state, or in a location where such function does not normally occur. Less basis to predict behavior = increased safety/effectiveness concerns.

### Metabolic vs. Structural vs. Reproductive Function
- **Metabolic products** (rely on viable functioning cells): sensitive to perturbations, may not retain function post-transplant, failure can have systemic/life-threatening effects. Generally require premarket approval UNLESS autologous or family-related use.
- **Structural products** (many rely on non-living tissue): relatively insensitive to external factors, limited safety concerns beyond local effects, long history of safe use. Lower regulatory burden.
- **Reproductive products:** less substantial rejection/compatibility issues, function normally in allogeneic settings, failure generally not life-threatening. Long history of medical use.

## Rationale for the Autologous/Family Exception (Criterion 4)

For metabolic cells/tissues that are minimally manipulated and for homologous use:
- **Autologous use** — lesser communicable disease concerns
- **First-degree blood relative use** — the agency considers it appropriate to leave the risk decision to the family and physician, even if donor tests positive
- These uses avoid the need for IND/BLA submissions as a policy matter

## Historical Context

- **1993:** Somatic cell and gene therapy statement — agency identified manipulation as trigger for premarket approval; cell selection initially considered more-than-minimal
- **1997:** Proposed Approach published — established the tiered framework and key definitions
- **1998:** Proposed rule for establishment registration and listing
- **2001:** Final rule — establishment registration and listing (66 FR 5447)
- **2004:** Final rules — donor eligibility (69 FR 29786), current good tissue practice/inspection/enforcement (69 FR 68612)
- **2005:** Full implementation of tiered risk-based approach
- **2017:** Final guidance on minimal manipulation/homologous use and same surgical procedure exception
- **2020:** Updated guidance extending enforcement discretion period

## The Tissue Reference Group (TRG)

FDA established the TRG (representatives from CBER and CDRH) to:
- Assist in jurisdictional decisions
- Apply consistent policy
- Provide manufacturers an informal process to obtain Agency recommendations on how 1271.10(a) criteria apply to their products

Formal classification decisions are available through the Request for Designation (RFD) process via the Office of Combination Products.
